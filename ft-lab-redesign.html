I was handed a product to reskin.
I chose to redesign it instead.

What began as a design system migration grew into a systematic, research-backed overhaul — challenging assumptions, rebuilding broken patterns, and delivering an experience grounded in actual user needs.

01Discovery · When the Brief Meets Reality

The brief said "design system migration."
The product said something else entirely.

When I first opened FT Lab, the ask felt simple: apply the new design system and ship. But the moment I moved through the product as a user — not a designer — something felt structurally wrong. I put down the file and went back to basics.

"We couldn't find all the existing design files. So instead of waiting, I took every screengrab I could get and rebuilt the user flow from scratch. That exercise alone told me more about the product than any documentation could."

— Nikhil Vemuri, FT Lab Redesign Audit

1
Screengrab the entire product
Every screen, every state, every modal. No design files meant no shortcuts — the live product in its current form became the single source of truth for the audit.
Audit
2
Reconstruct existing user flows manually
Using screengrabs, I mapped every navigation path, task entry point, and exit state. Gaps and dead-ends became visible for the first time. This wasn't documentation — it was diagnosis.
Flow Mapping
3
Cognitive walkthrough — act like a new user
Attempted key tasks without insider knowledge. Exposed where the product assumed too much, explained too little, and made users work unnecessarily hard.
Cognitive Walkthrough
4
Apply Nielsen's 10 heuristics systematically
Rated every screen against the 10 usability heuristics. Issues tagged by severity (1–4) and pattern type. What emerged wasn't a list of fixes — it was a structural argument for a proper redesign.
Heuristic Analysis
Navigation · Sev 4
Top tab overflow breaks below 1440px — critical features become invisible on standard enterprise monitors.
Consistency · Sev 3
Three different interaction patterns for the same action type across modules. No transferable mental model possible.
System Status · Sev 3
No feedback when long-running operations run. Users trigger actions twice, thinking the first didn't register.
Recognition vs Recall · Sev 2
Users must remember where features live. Zero contextual signposting. Discovery relies entirely on memory.
Flexibility · Sev 2
No keyboard shortcuts. No power-user pathways. Experts follow the exact same step count as beginners.
Error Prevention · Sev 3
Destructive actions are two clicks from any state. No undo pattern exists anywhere in the product.
Aesthetic · Sev 2
Equal visual weight on everything. No hierarchy — every element competes for full user attention simultaneously.
Help & Docs · Sev 2
Error messages are technical, not instructive. Zero guidance on what the user should do next.

"Apply the new design system to the existing product."

The Real Problem

02Problem Framing · Naming What's Broken

This wasn't a visual problem.
It was a structural one.

The heuristic audit gave me data. But data alone doesn't create alignment. I needed to frame the problem in a way that made the right path undeniable — for PMs, engineers, and the architect who hadn't budged yet.

Problem Statement
FT Lab users cannot efficiently navigate the product on standard enterprise hardware, and encounter inconsistent, cognitively demanding interactions that erode trust and slow task completion.
How Might We
How might we redesign FT Lab's navigation and interaction patterns so users can find features predictably, complete tasks confidently, and understand system state at all times?
Design Goals
Scalable navigation that works on all screen sizes. Consistent interaction patterns across all modules. Visual hierarchy that directs attention — not fragments it.

03The Conflict · When Data Meets Deadline

The architect said: "We can work around it.
Time is the constraint."

This is the moment that defines a designer's character. Not wireframe quality, not prototype fidelity. Whether you fold under pressure — or fight for the right outcome with the right evidence.

"I could feel the pull to just move on — apply the design system, ship something that looked new, and call it a win. But I'd seen the data. I knew what we'd be shipping. That wasn't a trade-off I was willing to make."

— Nikhil Vemuri, on the navigation redesign decision

⚡ Critical Decision Point — Navigation Architecture
01
The Constraint
Tight delivery deadline. Architect confident the tab overflow could be patched. "Let's not change what isn't broken beyond repair."
02
My Position
The audit showed navigation was broken on screens that the vast majority of enterprise users operate on daily. This was a critical usability failure, not cosmetic.
03
Research Collaboration
Partnered with the organisation's UX researcher. Ran task-based usability sessions. Collected behavioral data: navigation failure rates, time-on-task, error counts.
04
Evidence Presented
Brought findings to the architect — with the researcher in the room. Not as a debate. As evidence. Data doesn't argue. It shows.
05 ✓
Navigation Approved
Architect aligned. Side navigation greenlit. The redesign became a genuine UX overhaul — not a surface repaint.
📋
Task-Based Usability Testing
5 key tasks using the existing navigation. Time-on-task, errors, and abandonment tracked and compared against a side-nav prototype.
🗣️
Think-Aloud Protocol
Users verbalized navigation decisions in real time. Transcript revealed repeated confusion at tab overflow — users literally couldn't see where to go next.
📊
Behavioral Pattern Analysis
Product analytics reviewed for drop-off rates, most-used paths, and dead-end clicks. Data confirmed nav was the primary friction point.
🎯
Stakeholder Interviews
3 internal stakeholders interviewed. Mapped the delta between what they believed users needed versus what the research showed users actually did.
🔍
Comparative Analysis
Reviewed nav patterns across comparable enterprise tools. Side navigation was the dominant, proven pattern for feature-rich applications.
📌
Effort-Value Prioritization
All issues plotted on a 2×2 matrix. Navigation redesign landed firmly in high-value, high-feasibility quadrant — the priority was obvious.

04Solution · Rebuilding from the Foundations

Not one big redesign.
A structured rebuild, module by module.

With research backing and stakeholder alignment secured, the real work began. The strategy: don't redesign everything at once. Fix what's broken systematically, apply the design system consistently, and elevate UX at every touchpoint along the way.

1
Information Architecture Overhaul
Restructured the full IA before touching a single visual component. Grouped features by user mental models, not technical function. Reduced navigation depth from 4+ levels to max 3. Made the navigation redesign a logical extension, not an imposition.
IA
2
Navigation: Top Tabs → Hamburger Side Navigation
Replaced overflowing top tabs with a collapsible side nav. Persistent panel on wide screens, collapsed on smaller displays. Every navigation item always visible — no overflow, no hidden features, no broken hierarchy at any resolution.
Navigation
3
Component Standardisation Under the New Design System
Applied the new design system rigorously across every component. Crucially: each component was audited for usability first. Components that didn't support the right interaction model were redesigned before being built.
Design System
4
Cognitive Load Reduction — Screen by Screen
For every key screen: (1) What is the primary action? (2) Does the visual hierarchy reflect that? (3) What can be removed without losing function? Progressive disclosure replaced information dumping throughout.
UX Principles
5
Enterprise UX Principles Applied Throughout
Every decision filtered through: predictability, efficiency, error tolerance, and accessibility — the four pillars of UX that enterprise users depend on every single day.
UCD

06Outcome · What Actually Changed

A product that looks new.
An experience that works better.

The redesign wasn't measured in components migrated. It was measured in the gap between what users had to endure before, and what they experience now.

🧭
Navigation became predictable at every screen size
The side navigation resolved the overflow failure entirely. Every feature is accessible on every resolution. No hidden tabs, no dead-end paths. Users arrive at any enterprise setup and immediately know where things are.
🧠
Cognitive load reduced across every key workflow
Progressive disclosure, clear content hierarchy, and decluttered screens reduced the mental overhead of every key task. The design now does the orientation work — users don't have to.
🔧
Design system applied intentionally, not mechanically
The new design system was applied rigorously, but with judgement. Every component assessed for usability before it was built. The result is an implementation that's not just consistent — it's purposeful, with a principled foundation to extend.
📈
A replicable process for future redesigns
The screengrab audit → heuristic analysis → research collaboration → module-by-module rebuild became a documented approach. The next time a product needs redesigning, there's a proven playbook — not just a deliverable.

07Reflection · What This Project Taught Me

Good UX isn't about making things look better.
It's about making them work better.

This project didn't go the easy way. It went the right way. And the difference between those two paths is exactly what makes a designer worth hiring.

🔍
Reskins hide deeper UX problems
Every "visual redesign" brief is an invitation to look harder. The cosmetic layer is rarely the thing that's actually broken. Questioning the brief is the first thing that separates UX designers from visual designers.
📊
Data wins arguments better than opinions
The architect wasn't wrong to be cautious — caution is appropriate under deadline pressure. What changed his mind wasn't conviction. It was evidence. Research doesn't just inform design. It enables it.
⚡
Constraints force better thinking
No design files forced me to understand the product through a user's eyes. Tight deadlines forced prioritisation. Constraints aren't obstacles to good design — they're the conditions that require it.
🤝
Collaboration amplifies impact
One designer with a heuristic analysis is one voice. One designer plus a researcher with behavioral data is a business case. Know when you need amplification — and go find it.
🏗️
Structure before style, always
IA and navigation work happened before a single pixel of visual design was committed. You cannot decorate your way out of a structural problem. Foundation first, finish second.
🎯
User-centered means user-defended
Delivering UCD isn't just about what you put in the file. It's about what you're willing to defend in the room. Advocacy is a design skill — use it.
💡
The brief said "design system migration." The product said "I have structural problems." The user said "I can't find anything." A good designer listens to all three — and knows which one to fix first.

Products that look polished

FT Lab was a product that needed more than new paint. It needed someone willing to do the harder thing. This case study is proof that design conviction — grounded in research, defended under pressure, and executed with care — produces outcomes that matter.
